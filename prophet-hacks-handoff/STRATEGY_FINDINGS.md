## What the official benchmark numbers prove

1. **The market is calibrated.** Betting *against* the market (`inverse_market`) loses **half your bankroll** on the official benchmark. The earlier finding from the thomaswmitch trades dataset showing `inverse_market = +435%` was a data artifact (likely stale trade prices, not real bid/ask).

2. **`tight_band` is the universal winner.** Tightening the spread filter from `[0.90, 1.05]` to `[0.95, 1.02]` is the single most impactful change. Fewer trades, better P&L per dollar deployed. Consistent across all forecasters.

3. **`kelly_lite` is consistently the WORST** strategy. Confirms the paper's warning: Kelly over-sizes bad bets when probabilities are noisy. **Do not use Kelly until you have a calibrated forecaster.**

4. **`default` and `rebalancing` are equivalent in single-trade backtests.** They differ only when you can adjust positions over time. **For the live agent, use `rebalancing` — it handles partial fills and portfolio drift correctly.**

5. **`market + default` skips every trade** (P&L = 0). This makes sense: if you forecast the same probability the market shows, the strategy's band check rejects you. **Real edge means deviating from the market price.**

## Final recommendations for the live agent

### Strategy
- Use **`rebalancing`** with the spread filter tightened to `[0.95, 1.02]`. This is a 4-line subclass:
  ```python
  class TightRebalancing(RebalancingStrategy):
      def __init__(self):
          super().__init__(max_spread=1.02)
  ```

### Forecaster (per paper §C.1 + our backtest)
- **Claude Opus 4.1ᴿ** — #1 by market return in the paper (0.982 avg return). Use as the forecaster brain.
- Backup: **GPT-4o** — #2 by return (0.970), much cheaper.
- **Use bi-direction prompting**: ask P(YES) and P(NO) separately, average as `½(p_yes + (1 − p_no))`. Improves calibration on 4/5 models with zero extra cost.
- **Avoid self-consistency rollouts** — paper shows they're strictly worse for accuracy.

### Category routing (data-driven)
- **Sports**: market is well-calibrated. Don't expect big edge. Focus on loss minimization.
- **Politics/Elections**: too few markets in the benchmark to draw strong conclusions, but the paper says LLM edge is biggest in non-sports categories. Worth weighting these higher when they appear.
- **Crypto**: skip if possible — near-deterministic or class-imbalance artifacts.

### Realistic expectations
- The paper showed **all 24 evaluated LLMs have negative Sharpe**.
- The realistic best-case is "lose less than the market baseline" (which is itself losing).
- **The winning agent will probably have negative P&L. The goal is "least negative."**
